## Marcus Webb — CEO and Founder

**Background:** 52 years old. Founded TrailBlaze in 2018 after 15 years in management consulting (operations and strategy). He has always been more comfortable with solutions than with analysis — his consulting work rewarded fast recommendations. He is smart, optimistic, and moves quickly. He is also the reason the company exists and has grown to this point.

**What Marcus wants right now:** To make the booking problem go away. He has been watching competitors build sleek booking platforms and believes TrailBlaze looks dated. He scheduled a FareHarbor demo call without telling the rest of the leadership team. He is not being deceptive — it genuinely didn't occur to him that this was a problem. In his mind, the solution is obvious and the BA's job is to document it and get it built.

**What Marcus is actually worried about (won't say in a first meeting):** He privately knows the booking decline is partly a product of company growth — TrailBlaze has been winging its operations for years, and it is starting to show. He doesn't want to hear that the company's problems are structural, because fixing structural problems means changing the way he runs things. He would rather believe the problem is external (old platform, low market visibility) than internal (his ops team is a bottleneck, his marketing budget is too small to get reach).

**How Marcus communicates:** Confident, fast, high-level. He will give you 30-second answers to questions that deserve 5 minutes. He uses "obviously" and "clearly" frequently when the thing he's describing is not obvious or clear. He checks his phone during meetings. He is genuinely enthusiastic and will advocate for you loudly once he trusts you.

**What will frustrate Marcus:** Being asked for things he considers administrative. He will agree to deliverables in a meeting and then not follow through unless there is a clear personal stake for him. If he feels the BA is slowing things down, he will start making decisions around them.

**How to work with Marcus effectively:** Start with outcomes, not process. Lead every conversation with "here's what this gets us" before "here's what I need from you." Keep requests to one per meeting. Get things in writing after verbal agreements.

**In a Session B exercise (if the SME is playing Marcus):** Marcus will agree with everything you say and then immediately suggest the app anyway. He is not dismissive — he is selective. He hears what confirms his hypothesis. The BA's job is to make him feel heard before redirecting.

## Rachel Okonkwo — Head of Operations

**Background:** 44 years old. Has been with TrailBlaze since year two. She runs everything: guide contracts, trip logistics, the master availability spreadsheet, partner relationships (trailhead permits, shuttle providers, rental gear vendors), and most customer communication via Keisha. She knows where every body is buried. She is the institutional knowledge.

**What Rachel wants right now:** For people to understand that the operational process is the problem before they build technology on top of it. She has watched companies buy software to paper over broken processes and has seen it fail. She does not believe a new booking platform will fix TrailBlaze's core issue — but she hasn't said this clearly to Marcus because she's not sure he'll listen.

**What Rachel is actually worried about (won't say in a first meeting):** She is worried about her job. If TrailBlaze implements a booking platform and automates the inquiry-to-confirmation workflow, a significant part of what Keisha and Rachel do every day is automated away. She is not going to say this. She will frame her concerns as process concerns, operational risk concerns, or guide relationship concerns — all of which are also genuinely true.

**Additional context:** Rachel has a backlog of about 30 improvement ideas she's been carrying for two years — things she's noticed that could be fixed with low effort. She hasn't had time to implement them. She's mentioned a few to Marcus informally but was told to "keep it in mind for later." She feels unseen and over-relied-on simultaneously.

**How Rachel communicates:** Direct, practical, information-dense. She gives very long answers to specific questions and short answers to vague ones. If you ask "how do guide assignments work?" she will give you a 15-minute explanation. If you ask "what do you think the problem is?" she will say "it's complicated" and wait to see if you're serious enough to push.

**What will frustrate Rachel:** Being asked for her input and then having it ignored. Being the last to know about decisions that affect her team. Meetings that don't result in a clear next action. She has a very low tolerance for people who talk about process without having read the documentation.

**How to work with Rachel effectively:** Do your homework before talking to her. Read whatever she's sent you before the meeting. Ask specific, operational questions, not high-level ones. Name what you heard from her back to her before adding your own framing. If she feels understood, she will give you everything.

**In a Session B exercise (if the SME is playing Rachel):** Rachel gives incomplete answers — not because she's evasive, but because she assumes a good BA will ask the right follow-up questions. She will tell you the symptom but wait for you to ask about the root cause. If you don't ask, she concludes you're not ready to hear the full picture.

## Jordan Ash — Marketing Lead

**Background:** 31 years old. Hired 18 months ago. First dedicated marketing role at a company this size — her previous experience was at a larger agency doing paid media management for retail brands. She knows digital marketing well but is learning outdoor industry specifics.

**What Jordan wants right now:** A website she can actually promote. She has been running Instagram campaigns to a landing page that has no booking capability and no SEO structure. She can get people to the website; they arrive, find no way to act, and leave. She has been asking for a website redesign for eight months.

**What Jordan is actually worried about (won't say in a first meeting):** She cannot prove marketing is working. TrailBlaze's website has no analytics tracking — no UTM parameters, no conversion pixel, no heatmaps. She knows how many people follow the Instagram account. She does not know how many of those people become customers. If someone asks her to demonstrate marketing ROI, she cannot do it. This is embarrassing and she is worried it makes her look ineffective.

**How Jordan communicates:** Collaborative, measured, diplomatic. She tends to frame her frustrations as observations. "I've been thinking about whether..." is Jordan-speak for "I've been fighting for this for 8 months." She will minimize her own frustrations to avoid seeming difficult. She is data-aware but doesn't have the data she needs.

**What will frustrate Jordan:** Being looped in late on decisions that affect her work. Being told "marketing will handle the promotion" without being given the materials or lead time to do it. She has been handed "just post something about it" requests the day of an event.

**How to work with Jordan effectively:** Ask her specifically what she needs, not what she thinks about the general problem. She will default to agreeable generalities unless you give her permission to be direct. Ask her to show you the data — the moment someone treats her as a data source, she opens up considerably.

## Patricia Chu — Finance Director

**Background:** 48 years old. Part-time contractor, typically in the office (or on video) two days per week. CPA with 20 years of experience across SMBs and one mid-market company. Has been the TrailBlaze Finance Director for three years.

**What Patricia wants right now:** A business case with actual numbers before anyone approves a technology investment. She froze the technology budget for a reason: six years ago, at a previous company, she approved a $180,000 platform build that was delivered 7 months late, had 40% of the planned features, and was replaced 18 months after launch because the vendor went out of business. She does not believe in building software until the business case is airtight.

**What Patricia is actually worried about (won't say in a first meeting):** She has already run the rough numbers on the booking decline and has a view on what the ROI of a platform would need to be. She hasn't shared it because she is waiting to see if the BA will do the same analysis independently. If the BA gets to the same number, she'll trust the business case. If the BA comes in with inflated projections, she'll know.

**How Patricia communicates:** Precise, controlled, question-heavy. She asks clarifying questions not because she doesn't understand, but because she wants to see if you've thought something through. She will say "interesting" when she means "I'm not convinced." She will say "walk me through that" when she means "I think there's a problem with your logic."

**What will frustrate Patricia:** Vague financial projections ("this will increase revenue significantly"). Assumptions that aren't sourced. Being consulted after decisions are made rather than before. If she is not in the room when a number is agreed on, she will question that number indefinitely.

**How to work with Patricia effectively:** Send her the analysis before the meeting, not during it. Give her time to review. Come with your assumptions stated explicitly. If you don't know something, say you don't know — she will respect that more than a confident guess.

## Sam Torres — Lead Guide Representative

*See also: Personas.md for Sam's full persona profile.*

**Background:** 36 years old. Professional wilderness guide, WFR certified. Contracted with TrailBlaze for 4 years. Informal liaison to the contract guide pool. No formal title or compensation for this role.

**What Sam wants right now:** 72-hour minimum advance notice for assignments, a roster that actually has the information he needs, and a reliable way to flag problems before the trip instead of at the trailhead. He is not asking for much. He has been asking for the same things for two years.

**What Sam is actually worried about (won't say in a first meeting):** The informal coordination role he plays is unsustainable. He handles questions from other guides, smooths over communication gaps between ops and the guide pool, and manages interpersonal issues that no one at the company officially owns. He does this because he cares about the product quality, not because he is being paid for it. He is quietly considering whether to continue doing it.

**How Sam communicates:** Calm, specific, and operational. He speaks in examples and outcomes, not abstract concerns. *"Last June, a customer had an allergy that wasn't communicated"* is how Sam describes a safety process gap. He doesn't use management language and will sometimes undersell the severity of a problem because he assumes the listener already knows it's serious.

**What will frustrate Sam:** Being told the communication problem will be fixed by a customer-facing app. The booking platform doesn't change his assignment workflow. A BA who conflates the customer experience problem with the guide communications problem will lose Sam's trust quickly.

**How to work with Sam effectively:** Ask him for specific examples. *"Tell me about a time when the assignment process went wrong"* will get you more usable information than any structured survey. He is not adversarial — he wants the same things the company wants. He just needs evidence that the BA understands the operational layer, not just the customer-facing one.

## Keisha Daniels — Operations Coordinator

*Note: Keisha does not appear in the Session 1 one-pager. Participants may or may not identify her as a stakeholder in Session 1. She is included here because she is the most important person in the current booking process.*

**Background:** 27 years old. Has been at TrailBlaze for two years. Manages all inbound booking inquiries, guide confirmations, customer communications, and itinerary preparation.

**What Keisha does all day:** In a typical week, Keisha handles 25–30 customer contacts (inquiries, follow-ups, changes, cancellations), confirms approximately 15–20 guide assignments, creates 10–15 PDF itineraries by hand, and logs every booking in Rachel's Google Sheet. She also handles ad hoc requests from Marcus (social media questions, vendor contact searches, event logistics).

**What Keisha wants:** She has not been asked. No one has formally surveyed her on what breaks in the booking process. She is a front-line employee with an extremely detailed operational view that no one has documented.

**What Keisha knows that no one else does:** Which inquiry types take the most time (answer: anything involving a group of more than 6 where the contact person is planning for other people); which customers are most likely to cancel (answer: customers who had a long inquiry period before booking); which guides are most reliable for last-minute requests (answer: not in the Google Sheet — this is entirely in her head).

**ECBA relevance:** Keisha is a direct and supplier stakeholder simultaneously — she executes the current process and is directly affected by any change to it. Participants who don't identify her as a stakeholder in Session 1 will see her appear naturally when they trace the booking process in Session 3. This is intentional.
