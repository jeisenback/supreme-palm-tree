Welcome to the TrailBlaze ECBA Study Series: Five Months to ECBA Readiness

The IIBA East Tennessee Chapter runs this series as part of its mission to make business analysis accessible to all. Whether you’re working toward your first certification or deepening a practice you’ve already built, this program is designed around you.

This program runs April through August — one 90-minute session per month, one case study that evolves across all five sessions, and a structured study plan to carry you to exam day.

Why this series
The ECBA (Entry Certificate in Business Analysis) is a scenario-based exam that tests how you think, not just what you’ve memorized. Most study approaches get this backwards. This series is built around a single evolving case study — TrailBlaze Adventures — so every concept you learn lands in a real situation, not an abstract definition. By the time you sit for the exam, you’ll have worked through the same kinds of problems the exam tests, not just read about them.

This handout covers everything you need to set yourself up before Session 1 begins.

Preparing to Study

How you take care of yourself outside sessions directly affects what you can absorb inside them. These are not side notes — they compound with everything else you do.

- Sleep: maintain a consistent sleep schedule. Memory consolidation happens during sleep. Late-night cramming costs more than it gains.
- Exercise: aim for 2.5 hours of cardio and 2 days of strength training per week. Physical activity measurably improves focus and retention.
- Hydration: keep water with you while studying. Even mild dehydration degrades cognitive performance.
- Nutrition: prioritize fruits, seeds, oatmeal, green tea, dark chocolate. Minimize high-sugar, high-processed-food periods before study sessions.
- Screen discipline: put your phone away at least 1 hour before bed. Blue light and notification checks fragment the sleep onset that study depends on.

Study session structure — Pomodoro method:
1. Focus for 25 minutes (no interruptions)
2. Break for 5 minutes
3. Repeat 4 times
4. After 4 rounds, take a 15-minute break
5. Repeat

How to Take Notes — Cornell Method

Cornell notes work for lectures, reading, and session content. Use the same format consistently so reviewing is fast.

Structure of a Cornell notes page:
- Notes column (right, ~70% of page): capture the gist of what you've learned and important details in your own words — not verbatim transcription
- Cue column (left, ~30% of page): add questions that help you organize or remember the material; fill this in during or immediately after taking notes; use it to identify what you still need to study
- Summary section (bottom, 3–5 lines): write what you learned in no more than three sentences; do this while the information is still fresh; start every subsequent study session by reading this section first

Apply this to:
- BABOK chapter reading (required before each session)
- Session content during the 90-minute block
- Homework MCQ review

How to Study — Techniques That Work

Spending 10 minutes reviewing every evening beats one long session per week. Use these methods:

Retrieval practice (highest impact)
- Before reviewing your notes, write or sketch everything you can remember from memory
- Put it in your own words; come up with your own examples; connect it to your work or life
- Then open your notes and check what you got right — the gap is what to study next
- Tools: flashcards, blank-page recall, concept maps

Dual coding
- Combine words with visuals — sketch your notes, draw the BACCM as a diagram, map stakeholders spatially
- Visual + verbal encoding creates stronger memory traces than text alone

Interleaving
- Don't study one topic until you've "finished" it — alternate between three related topics in the same session
- Example: BACCM → stakeholder types → requirements vs. designs → back to BACCM
- Interleaving creates the forgetting-and-retrieval cycle that builds durable memory

Elaboration
- Works best when you already have some prior knowledge
- Ask "how does this work?" and "why does this matter?" for every concept
- Explain concepts to yourself out loud
- Compare similar concepts: "How is a need different from a solution? How is value different from a benefit?"

Teaching others
- If you can explain it clearly, you know it
- Use the case study materials — try explaining TrailBlaze's situation in BACCM terms to a colleague or peer

ECBA Exam Overview

The ECBA (Entry Certificate in Business Analysis) is IIBA’s foundational certification.

- 50 scenario-based multiple choice questions
- Tests the 9 ECBA domains (not BABOK knowledge areas directly, but they map closely)
- BABOK (Business Analysis Body of Knowledge) is the reference framework — your reading schedule follows it
- No penalty for wrong answers — answer every question
- Questions test application, not recall — every stem describes a situation and asks what the BA should do
- Tip: eliminate options that describe project management activities, implementation tasks, or solution-jumping behavior — these are almost always wrong
