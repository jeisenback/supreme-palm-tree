# TrailBlaze Adventures — Stakeholder Interview Excerpts (Session 2)

--- DOCUMENT GUIDE ---
What this is:   Edited excerpts from the five initial stakeholder interviews conducted by the BA in the first week of the engagement. These are realistic, not clean — stakeholders contradict each other, give incomplete information, and sometimes answer a different question than the one asked.
Who it's for:   Participants (Session 2 reveal).
When to use it: Release at the ARCS bridge in Session 2A — after the homework share-out and before concept instruction. Participants use these transcripts in Session 2 exercises to identify root causes, plan their BA approach, and develop their elicitation strategy.
Facilitator note: Do not clean up or summarize these for participants. Part of the exercise is learning to pull structured information from unstructured conversation.
--- END GUIDE ---

---

**How to read these excerpts:**

Each interview was 45–60 minutes. These are edited excerpts — important passages, not full transcripts. Filler conversation, scheduling logistics, and off-topic sections have been cut.

As you read, pay attention to:
- What is being said vs. what is being claimed
- Where stakeholders agree and where they contradict each other
- What information is missing that you would need to confirm the root cause
- What each person seems to be worried about that they aren't saying directly

## Interview 1 — Marcus Webb, CEO and Founder
*Interview conducted: Week 1, Tuesday. Location: Marcus's office.*

---

**BA:** I appreciate you taking the time. I want to start by understanding the situation from your perspective — not just the symptom, but the context. When did you first notice the booking numbers dropping?

**Marcus:** Honestly? Keisha flagged it. She said something like, "the Julys are looking slow" — this was last August. I looked at the numbers and yeah, we're down significantly from where we were. It's not a crisis yet, but it's a trend and trends become crises.

**BA:** What do you think is driving it?

**Marcus:** The platform. I mean, that's obvious, right? Look at how we book trips. A customer sends an email, waits two days, gets a reply, sends another email. That's a 2015 experience. Every competitor of ours has a real booking flow. FareHarbor, Xola, Peek Pro — these are tools built for exactly what we do. We're losing customers in the inquiry window before they even talk to us.

**BA:** Have you seen data that points to customers dropping off in that inquiry window specifically?

**Marcus:** Not directly, no. We don't have that kind of tracking. But it's the obvious explanation. If I'm a customer and I have to wait three days for a reply, I'm going to find someone who can answer me immediately.

**BA:** What would success look like for you at the end of this engagement?

**Marcus:** A booking platform. Something customers can use to find our trips, pick a date, pay, and get a confirmation — all without calling us. If I can do that in the next four months, I'll be happy.

**BA:** And what would happen if we got a booking platform but the bookings didn't recover?

**Marcus:** *(pause)* I'd want to understand why. But I don't think that's going to happen.

**BA:** One more thing. Rachel seems to believe the issue is partly about guide availability — that we're turning away requests because guides aren't confirmed in time. Is that consistent with what you're seeing?

**Marcus:** Rachel runs a really tight ship. She's incredible. But I think what she's describing is a symptom of not having the right tools, not a root cause. If we had a system that showed guide availability in real time, she wouldn't have that problem.

**BA:** Has the technology budget been formally frozen?

**Marcus:** Patricia froze it, yes. We need a business case. I told her we'd have one. That's partly why you're here.

## Interview 2 — Rachel Okonkwo, Head of Operations
*Interview conducted: Week 1, Thursday. Location: Operations office (open plan; some interruptions).*

---

**BA:** Walk me through what a typical week looks like from your seat.

**Rachel:** *(leans back)* Okay, so. Keisha handles the front end of bookings — inquiries, confirmations, the itinerary PDFs. I handle everything that happens after confirmation: guide assignment, logistics, permit coordination, vendor contacts. We work closely. If something goes wrong on the customer side, I'm usually the one who has to go fix it.

**BA:** How many bookings are you managing at any given time?

**Rachel:** Peak season? We might have 15 to 20 active bookings in a 2-week window. Off-peak, more like 8 to 10. Plus the multi-day packages, which are heavier to manage.

**BA:** The booking decline — what's your read on what's driving it?

**Rachel:** It's not one thing. That's what I keep trying to say. Marcus thinks it's the website. Jordan thinks it's marketing. Maybe both of those are contributing. But what I know for sure — because I can see it — is that we're turning bookings away. Not many. But we are.

**BA:** Can you describe how that happens?

**Rachel:** Customer contacts us, wants a specific weekend. I check the spreadsheet — that trail has two guides who can do it. I reach out to both. One is already booked that weekend, I forgot to update the sheet. Other one says he can probably do it but needs to confirm by Wednesday. Customer is waiting. Wednesday comes, guide confirms. I have Keisha send the booking confirmation. By then it's Thursday and the customer booked with someone else.

**BA:** How often does that happen?

**Rachel:** I don't have an exact number. Honestly, I've never tracked it. My guess? Once or twice a month in peak season. Maybe more.

**BA:** So at current booking rates, that could be 2–4% of your peak volume.

**Rachel:** Roughly, yes. It adds up over the season.

**BA:** Is the guide availability issue something that's gotten worse over time?

**Rachel:** *(quiet for a moment)* The guide pool has changed. We've had turnover. Some of our most experienced guides — people who would pick up assignments on short notice because they knew our routes — they've moved on. The newer guides need more lead time. They're not as familiar with the trails and they want to prepare properly. Which is completely reasonable. But it changes the operational timeline.

**BA:** What would make the biggest difference for you operationally?

**Rachel:** More lead time on confirmations. If I knew 5 days out instead of 48 hours, I could manage the guide assignment process with a lot less scrambling. And a better way to communicate changes to guides — right now that's all text messages and they don't always get read.

**BA:** Would a new booking platform solve those problems?

**Rachel:** It would solve the customer-facing part. The guide assignment piece is different. That's internal. A customer booking platform doesn't tell a guide that the customer count changed.

---
*[Interview interrupted for 8 minutes — vendor call for Rachel. Continued below.]*
---

**BA:** Before the interruption, you mentioned guide turnover. Have you talked to Marcus about that specifically?

**Rachel:** I've mentioned it. He knows guides have left. I don't think he connects it to the booking decline the way I do.

**BA:** Do you think there's a connection?

**Rachel:** *(long pause)* I think customer experience and guide experience are the same thing. If a guide doesn't know what they're walking into and they have a bad trip, they're less likely to take our assignments. If we lose the good guides, customer experience suffers. It's a loop.

## Interview 3 — Jordan Ash, Marketing Lead
*Interview conducted: Week 1, Friday. Location: Video call (Jordan working from home).*

---

**BA:** Thanks for making time. Tell me how you see the booking situation from the marketing side.

**Jordan:** So, the thing I keep coming back to is discoverability. If someone in Charlotte has never heard of us and they search for "weekend hiking trip Asheville," we're not showing up on the first page. We're barely showing up at all. A competitor that started in 2021 is outranking us for most of the keywords we should own. That's a problem.

**BA:** How do you know this?

**Jordan:** I've done some basic keyword analysis with free tools. I can't afford SEMrush or anything substantial — that's been an ongoing budget conversation — but the signal is clear. We have almost no domain authority. The website hasn't been updated with fresh content in over a year.

**BA:** Can you put a scale on the lost traffic? How many potential customers might not be finding you?

**Jordan:** No. And that's part of the problem. We don't have any tracking on the website. No Google Analytics, no UTM parameters on my Instagram link in bio. I know how many people follow our account and roughly how much engagement our posts get. I don't know how many of those people try to book a trip. If I could show that number to Marcus, I'd have a much stronger case for the marketing budget.

**BA:** What would you need to make that case?

**Jordan:** Analytics on the website, first of all. A landing page that actually does something when someone clicks through from Instagram — right now it's an info page with a "contact us" email link. If they have to email us, I can't track whether they did. A real booking flow would give me conversion data I could use.

**BA:** Is the search issue related to the platform question, or is it separate?

**Jordan:** Both, honestly. You can have better SEO without a new platform — you just need fresh content and some technical cleanup. That's something we could do now, relatively cheaply. But a real booking platform would also help because it gives me pages to promote that actually convert.

**BA:** Have you had any conversations with Rachel or Marcus about the SEO issue specifically?

**Jordan:** I've sent Marcus a summary doc three times. He always says "great, let's pick this up when things slow down." *(short laugh)* Things don't really slow down. Rachel and I don't interact much on strategy — she's usually heads-down on operations.

**BA:** Do you know if any customers have mentioned finding competitors online rather than TrailBlaze?

**Jordan:** No. We don't have exit surveys or anything like that. We have NPS after trips — those scores are actually pretty good, which is encouraging. But NPS only reaches customers who already completed a trip. It doesn't tell us anything about the customers who found a competitor instead.

## Interview 4 — Patricia Chu, Finance Director
*Interview conducted: Week 2, Monday. Location: Video call.*

---

**BA:** I wanted to get your view on the situation before we go too far down any path. You froze the technology budget — can you walk me through your reasoning?

**Patricia:** I froze discretionary capital expenditure. That's different from freezing all technology spend. If Marcus wants to renew a SaaS subscription or buy a laptop, that's fine. What I froze is any new platform investment above $10,000.

**BA:** What prompted that specifically?

**Patricia:** I've seen this before. Company has a performance dip. Someone suggests a technology investment. The investment gets approved before the root cause is confirmed. The technology doesn't fix the underlying problem. Now you have the original problem plus the cost of a platform you don't fully use. I didn't want that to happen here.

**BA:** What would a business case need to include to get your approval?

**Patricia:** I want to see the revenue impact of the current problem quantified — not estimated, quantified from our actual data. I want to see a realistic projection for how a platform would recover that revenue, with assumptions stated clearly. I want to see total cost of ownership including implementation and ongoing fees, not just the build cost. And I want to see an analysis of alternatives — have we considered options other than building a platform? What does a lower-cost solution look like?

**BA:** Have you done any preliminary analysis yourself?

**Patricia:** I've looked at the numbers, yes.

**BA:** Would you be willing to share your analysis so we're working from the same baseline?

**Patricia:** *(pause)* Once you've done your own version, yes. I'd like to compare the two independently before we merge them. If we both get to the same number from different directions, that's a stronger case than if we just used mine.

**BA:** One more question — do you have a view on what's causing the booking decline?

**Patricia:** I have a hypothesis. I think it's a combination of factors. The inquiry-to-confirmation timeline is too long and some customers are dropping out. We're probably also losing customers who find us through search, or rather, don't find us through search. And the guide situation — I'm aware of the turnover and I think that's affecting the reliability of the product in ways that aren't fully visible yet.

**BA:** Has anyone else articulated it that way?

**Patricia:** I'm not sure. I haven't had a structured conversation with the rest of the team about root cause. I've heard the symptoms from each person separately.

## Interview 5 — Sam Torres, Lead Guide Representative
*Interview conducted: Week 2, Wednesday. Location: Phone call; Sam was driving between sites.*

---

**BA:** I appreciate you taking the call. I know you're not officially a staff member, so I especially want to make sure the guide perspective is in the picture from the start.

**Sam:** Yeah, no, I appreciate it. Not everyone thinks to call us.

**BA:** Tell me about the assignment process from your end.

**Sam:** So Keisha texts me. Usually something like "Saturday the 14th, Black Balsam, six people." I say yes or no. If I say yes, that's it until a second text the day before with the roster. The roster is usually first names and where they're from. Sometimes a fitness note. That's about it.

**BA:** What information would you want that you're not currently getting?

**Sam:** Honestly? Medical stuff first. I've had two situations in the past 18 months where a customer had a condition I should have known about. One was an allergy I found out about at the trailhead. One was a cardiovascular issue — customer told me they had it when I asked about their fitness level during the intro. Neither of these was in my briefing. I carry gear for those situations, but I should know in advance.

**BA:** How did you find out about the allergy?

**Sam:** Customer mentioned it. Just casually, like they assumed I knew. I stopped and dealt with it immediately, but it was not how that should go.

**BA:** What's the minimum lead time you'd need to do your job well?

**Sam:** For a trail I know well, 48 hours is workable. For a trail I haven't done recently, or a group with special requirements, I want at least 72 hours. Ideally a week for complex groups — large groups, young kids, physical limitations.

**BA:** What do you hear from other guides? Are they having similar experiences?

**Sam:** Yeah, it's a common complaint. The notification thing is the number one thing. Guys find out late, can't prep, get there and something's different from what they expected. A couple of guys who left in the past couple years — it was partly that. Not just the money. The money is fine. It's more the feeling that you're just a resource being deployed, not a professional being engaged.

**BA:** If TrailBlaze built a new booking system, what would you need it to do from the guide side?

**Sam:** Honestly, the customer booking part doesn't really affect me. What affects me is how the assignment information flows. I want to know: who's confirmed, what are their fitness levels and any medical flags, what gear are they bringing, what's the meeting point, what's the cancellation situation. And if anything changes, I want a notification — not a text that says "quick update," something clear.

**BA:** Has anyone at TrailBlaze asked for your input on this before?

**Sam:** *(short laugh)* No. You're the first.

## Notes for Session 2 Exercises

The five interviews above contain the following types of information — part of your job is to sort them:

- **Statements of opinion presented as fact** (note where stakeholders say "obvious" or "clearly" or "I think")
- **Claims that require data to verify** (note where someone says something you cannot confirm from what you have)
- **Contradictions between stakeholders** (note where two people describe the same situation differently)
- **Information gaps** (note what a stakeholder could not or would not tell you)
- **Signals about what stakeholders are worried about** (not just what they said, but what they seemed to be protecting)

You do not need to resolve all of these in Session 2. You need to identify them.
