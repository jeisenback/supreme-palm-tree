CHECKPOINT QUESTIONS (pause during slides — vote, then discuss)

Checkpoint 1 — After BACCM slide
Q: Which element of the BACCM describes the circumstances that influence, are influenced by, and provide understanding of the change?

A) Change
B) Need
C) Context
D) Value

Answer: C
Rationale: Context encompasses the environmental, organizational, and situational circumstances surrounding the change. Change is the act of transformation; Need is the problem or opportunity; Value is the worth or usefulness. Context is the "why now and why here" of any initiative.

---

Checkpoint 2 — After Stakeholders slide
Q: A BA identifies a stakeholder who has high influence over the project outcome but low interest in it. What is the most appropriate engagement approach?

A) Exclude them from communication to avoid overwhelming them
B) Send frequent detailed updates to build their interest
C) Keep them informed at a high level and monitor their position
D) Make them the project sponsor to increase their engagement

Answer: C
Rationale: High influence / low interest stakeholders should be kept satisfied — informed at a summary level without being overwhelmed. Their position can shift, so the BA should monitor them. Excluding them risks being blindsided; flooding them with detail is counterproductive; forcing a sponsor role doesn't address the engagement challenge.

PRACTICE ROUND (end of session — 4 questions, 2 minutes each)

Practice 1
Q: An operations director tells a BA: "We've decided to install a new digital trail kiosk system at all trailheads. Your job is to document the requirements for it." What should the BA do first?

A) Begin documenting requirements for the kiosk system as directed
B) Create a stakeholder map for the kiosk implementation
C) Question whether kiosks are the right solution by first defining the underlying need
D) Escalate to the sponsor that the solution was chosen prematurely

Answer: C
Rationale: The BA's work starts with defining the need — not accepting a prescribed solution. The operations director has jumped to a solution without establishing whether it addresses the real need. A BA who documents requirements for the wrong solution is still doing the wrong work. This doesn't require escalation; it requires asking the right questions.

---

Practice 2
Q: A new self-serve booking feature was launched at TrailBlaze. Weekend bookings increased 22% in the first month. However, the Lead Guide reports that assignment errors have increased and two guides have resigned. What does this reveal about value delivery?

A) The solution was successful — the primary booking KPI was exceeded
B) The solution delivered partial value; the full business need included operational stability, not just booking volume
C) The BA should have prevented the launch until guide management issues were resolved
D) Guide attrition is an HR matter outside the scope of BA value assessment

Answer: B
Rationale: Value in BA is assessed across all affected stakeholders, not just the sponsoring executive's primary metric. A 22% booking increase represents value for the CEO and Marketing Lead, but the decline in guide performance and retention signals the full business need was broader than defined. BAs must assess value holistically — across all stakeholders who have a relationship to the change. Selecting C would be overreach; D is incorrect because guide attrition directly affects the change outcome and is clearly within the BA's value assessment scope.

---

Practice 3
Q: According to the BACCM, which element must be clearly defined before a solution can be appropriately recommended?

A) Value
B) Context
C) Need
D) Stakeholder

Answer: C
Rationale: The BACCM defines Solution as "a set of changes to enable the enterprise to meet the need." You cannot define a solution without first defining the need. This is a foundational ECBA principle and one of the most frequently tested concepts.

---

Practice 4
Q: Two key stakeholders have conflicting views on what the project should achieve. What does this most likely indicate?

A) The project scope is too broad and should be reduced
B) One stakeholder is incorrect and should be overruled by the sponsor
C) The BA should escalate the conflict to the steering committee immediately
D) The need has not been clearly defined and requires further analysis

Answer: D
Rationale: Conflicting stakeholder views on project goals typically signal that the underlying need has not been articulated clearly enough. When the need is ambiguous, different stakeholders fill the gap with their own assumptions. The BA's role is to facilitate alignment around a shared need definition — not to pick sides or escalate prematurely.

Homework 1
Q: What is the primary purpose of business analysis according to BABOK?

A) To manage project timelines and budgets on behalf of the project manager
B) To enable change in an enterprise by defining needs and recommending solutions that deliver value to stakeholders
C) To document requirements exactly as described by the project sponsor
D) To ensure technical solutions are implemented correctly by the development team

Answer: B
Rationale: This is the Business Analysis Body of Knowledge (BABOK) definition. BA is about enabling change and delivering value — not project management, passive documentation, or implementation oversight. If you see "project management" or "ensure technical implementation" in an answer option, it is almost always a distractor.

---

Homework 2
Q: Which of the following best describes the BA mindset principle of "thinking holistically"?

A) Considering all available technical options before recommending a solution
B) Reviewing the full codebase before writing requirements
C) Understanding how a change impacts the broader enterprise, not just the immediate project scope
D) Involving every stakeholder equally in every decision

Answer: C
Rationale: Thinking holistically means considering how a change ripples across the organization — processes, people, technology, and strategy — beyond the immediate scope. It does not mean involving everyone equally or reviewing technical artifacts; it means understanding systemic impact.

---

Homework 3
Q: In the BACCM, which element represents the worth, importance, or usefulness of something to a stakeholder?

A) Need
B) Solution
C) Change
D) Value

Answer: D
Rationale: Value is defined in the BACCM as the worth, importance, or usefulness of something. It can be tangible (revenue, cost reduction) or intangible (customer trust, brand reputation). Need is the problem or opportunity; Solution is the set of changes; Change is the act of transformation.

---

Homework 4
Q: A business case reads: "We need to launch a mobile app to increase sales by 20%." What is the main problem with this statement from a BA perspective?

A) The 20% target is too ambitious for a mobile initiative
B) It conflates a solution (mobile app) with the need and skips root cause analysis
C) It lacks a project timeline and budget estimate
D) It does not identify the project sponsor by name

Answer: B
Rationale: "Launch a mobile app" is a solution, not a need. A well-formed need statement describes the problem, who is affected, and the measurable impact — without prescribing how to solve it. Timeline, budget, and sponsor are legitimate project concerns but not what's wrong with this statement from a BA standpoint.

---

Homework 5
Q: Which of the following is NOT a core BA competency as described in BABOK?

A) Facilitation
B) Critical thinking
C) Software development
D) Negotiation

Answer: C
Rationale: Core BA competencies include facilitation, critical thinking, negotiation, communication, and documentation. Software development is a technical/delivery discipline — not a BA competency. BAs collaborate with technical teams but are not expected to build solutions.

---

Homework 6
Q: A BA learns that recent data privacy regulations have changed how customer data can be stored and shared. This information is most relevant to which BACCM element?

A) Need
B) Change
C) Stakeholder
D) Context

Answer: D
Rationale: Context includes the circumstances that influence the change initiative — regulatory environment, market conditions, organizational culture, and competing priorities. The regulation is a contextual factor that shapes what solutions are viable, not the need itself or the change being made.
