Slide 4 — ARCS Opener [0:03 — 7 min]
- ATTENTION: A mountain adventure company spent $280K on GPS tracking devices for their guides. After the rollout, the devices sat in storage. Guides said the units were too bulky for technical terrain and drained battery mid-trip. No one had asked the guides what they actually needed on the trail. The operations director had defined the solution before anyone confirmed what the problem was.
- RELEVANCE: TrailBlaze Adventures is about to make the same mistake. Weekend bookings are down 18%. Leadership is already talking about building a new mobile app — but has anyone actually asked why customers stopped booking, or why guides are leaving?
- CONFIDENCE: Today we'll use the BACCM and a stakeholder template to plant a flag on the real problem before anyone picks a route.
- SATISFACTION: By the end of this session, your group will have a BACCM chart, a stakeholder list, and a problem statement — the base camp everything else is built from.

Slide 5 — What is Business Analysis? (ECBA Domain 1) [0:10 — 6 min]
- BA is the practice of enabling change in an enterprise by defining needs and recommending solutions that deliver value to stakeholders.
- The BA is not the gear manager — they are the route planner who confirms the team is climbing the right mountain before anyone laces their boots.
- Key roles: analyst, facilitator, communicator, critical thinker
- TrailBlaze anchor: Right now, no one at TrailBlaze is playing this role. Leadership is pointing at a summit ("build a platform") without checking whether it's the right mountain. That gap — between symptom and confirmed need — is exactly where the BA works.

Slide 6 — BA Mindset (ECBA Domain 2) [0:16 — 6 min]
- Values: communication, curiosity, accountability, adaptability
- Principles: think holistically, focus on outcomes not outputs, challenge assumptions
- Competencies: facilitation, critical thinking, negotiation, documentation
- TrailBlaze anchor: The CEO, Head of Ops, and Marketing Lead are each pointing at a different trailhead. A good BA doesn't pick one and start hiking — they read the terrain first, then help the team agree on the right route.
- Exam tip: Mindset is a standalone ECBA domain — it is tested independently

Slide 7 — The BACCM [0:22 — 6 min]
- Change: an act of transformation in response to a need
- Need: a problem or opportunity to be addressed
- Solution: a set of changes to enable the enterprise to meet the need
- Stakeholder: a group or individual with a relationship to the change
- Value: the worth, importance, or usefulness of something
- Context: the circumstances that influence, are influenced by, and provide understanding of the change
- Mnemonic: C-N-S-S-V-C (Change, Need, Solution, Stakeholder, Value, Context)
- Exam tip: Every ECBA question can be framed through one or more BACCM elements

Slide 9 — Assessing Value [0:35 — 5 min]
- Value is not just revenue — it is the worth, importance, or usefulness of something to a stakeholder
- Tangible value: bookings, revenue, cost reduction, time saved
- Intangible value: customer trust, guide satisfaction, brand reputation, team confidence
- The BA's role: not just delivering a solution, but verifying the solution delivers value to the right stakeholders
  - At TrailBlaze, the CEO measures value in bookings. The Head of Ops measures it in guide retention. Marketing measures it in brand reach. Finance measures it in return on investment (ROI). A BA must assess value for all of them.
- Key question: "Who defines success, and what does success actually look like for each stakeholder?"
  - TrailBlaze anchor: If bookings increase 20% but two guides quit and Net Promoter Score (NPS) drops, was that value delivered?
- Exam tip: ECBA questions on value often test whether you can identify whose definition of value matters and whether a proposed solution would actually deliver it

Slide 10 — Key Stakeholder Concepts [0:40 — 6 min]
- Stakeholder types: sponsor, domain SME, end user, regulator, supplier
- Stakeholder analysis: interest level, influence level, what is at stake
- RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed): a framework for assigning stakeholder roles in decisions
- TrailBlaze landscape: CEO, Head of Ops, Marketing Lead, Finance Director, Lead Guide, Weekend Travelers, Legal/Compliance
- Exam tip: Understand stakeholder categories and how a BA engages each type

Slide 11 — Writing a Problem Statement [0:46 — 4 min]
- On an expedition, a vague problem statement is the difference between "we're off course" and "we missed the summit window by 3 hours — here is why and what it cost us."
- A good problem statement: names who is affected, describes the problem, states the measurable impact, avoids proposing a route
- Bad: "TrailBlaze needs a new booking platform to fix declining sales"
  - Why it fails: proposes a route, not measurable, skips root cause
- Good: "TrailBlaze Adventures has experienced an 18% decline in weekend bookings over two quarters. The root cause is unclear; competing diagnoses exist across leadership. Without confirming the real need, any solution risks climbing the wrong mountain."
- Exam tip: If a problem statement contains a solution, it is not a problem statement
