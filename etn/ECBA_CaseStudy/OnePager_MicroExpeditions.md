## The Company

TrailBlaze Adventures is a guided outdoor adventure company based in Asheville, North Carolina. Founded in 2018 by Marcus Webb, the company runs guided trips ranging from half-day hikes to week-long backcountry expeditions in the southern Appalachians and surrounding areas.

The business targets two main customer groups: **urban professionals** (day-trippers and weekend adventurers from Charlotte, Atlanta, and the Research Triangle) and **small family groups** seeking low-logistics outdoor experiences. Most customers find TrailBlaze through word of mouth or Instagram.

**Scale:**
- 12 full-time staff
- ~40 contracted guides (seasonal, per-trip; not employees)
- ~3,500 Instagram followers; no paid advertising
- No functional online booking — customers call or email to book

**Trip types and approximate pricing (per person):**
| Trip type | Price | Duration | Typical group size |
|-----------|-------|----------|--------------------|
| Half-day hike | $65 | 4–5 hours | 4–8 people |
| Full-day guided trip | $95 | 8–9 hours | 4–8 people |
| Weekend multi-day | $320 | 2 nights / 3 days | 4–10 people |
| Week-long expedition | $875 | 5–7 days | 6–12 people |

Weekend multi-day packages are the core product — they represent about **60% of total revenue**.

## How a Booking Works Today

There is no online booking system. The current process has not changed since the company launched.

1. A customer sees a post on Instagram or hears about TrailBlaze from a friend. They want to book a weekend trip.
2. They email or call the TrailBlaze office. If they call, they usually reach **Keisha Daniels** (Operations Coordinator) or leave a voicemail.
3. Keisha checks a Google Sheet (maintained by Rachel Okonkwo, Head of Operations) to see whether a guide is available for the requested date and trail.
4. Keisha replies to the customer: *"We have availability on [date] for the [trail] trip. Our guide for that weekend will be [name]. Would you like to confirm?"*
5. If the customer says yes, Keisha texts or emails the assigned guide to confirm.
6. The customer pays by Venmo, Zelle, or personal check. No credit card processing currently.
7. Keisha manually creates a PDF itinerary and emails it to the customer. This takes 10–20 minutes per booking.
8. The booking is logged in the Google Sheet.

**What happens when things go wrong:**
- If a guide cancels within 48 hours of a trip, Keisha has to call through a list of available guides to find a replacement. This fails approximately **8% of the time** — meaning the trip is rescheduled or refunded. This rate was about **3%** two years ago.
- If a customer has questions about a trip after booking (packing list, meeting point, weather), they email or call Keisha again. There is no self-serve way to access their itinerary.
- Keisha handles roughly 25–30 customer contacts per week across bookings, follow-ups, and cancellations. She has said she cannot take on more volume without dropping something.

## The Numbers

**Peak season (June–August):** approximately **60–65 weekend bookings per month**

**Current run rate (past two quarters):** approximately **53 weekend bookings per month**

That is an **18% decline** — roughly 10–12 fewer weekend bookings per month than the same period last year.

At an average booking value of approximately **$250 per booking** (mix of group sizes and trip types), that is roughly **$30,000–$36,000 in lost annualized revenue** — before accounting for any multi-day packages that were not booked because a potential customer couldn't get through the inquiry process.

TrailBlaze does not track the following metrics formally:
- How many customers inquire but never book (inquiry-to-booking conversion rate)
- How many customers who had a cancelled trip re-booked vs. did not return
- How many customers found TrailBlaze through search vs. referral vs. social media
- Guide assignment error rate (Rachel estimates this informally)

## The Presenting Symptom

Bookings are down. Leadership noticed three months ago. No one has formally analyzed why.

Here is where each leader currently stands:

**Marcus Webb — CEO and Founder**
Marcus founded TrailBlaze after a 15-year career in management consulting. He reads tech and startup news regularly and has been looking at competitor platforms. He believes the booking experience is the problem. *"We look like a company from 2015. Customers expect to be able to book online in 3 clicks. We're making them call us."* He has already had a demo call with FareHarbor (a booking platform used by outdoor tour operators) without notifying the rest of the leadership team.

**Rachel Okonkwo — Head of Operations**
Rachel joined TrailBlaze in year two and effectively runs all logistics. She manages guide contracts, trip scheduling, the master availability Google Sheet, and most customer communication through Keisha. She believes the guide availability problem is the real issue. *"We turn away bookings. I know we do. When guides aren't available, I can't confirm the trip — so the customer waits, and sometimes they book somewhere else before I can get back to them."* She has not shared her full assessment with Marcus.

**Jordan Ash — Marketing Lead**
Jordan was hired 18 months ago and built the Instagram account from ~500 to 3,500 followers. She is aware that TrailBlaze has almost no search presence — a competitor that launched in 2021 ranks ahead of them for most search terms TrailBlaze should own. *"We don't show up in Google. If someone who doesn't already know us searches for 'weekend hiking trips Asheville,' we're not there. The booking drop might partly be because we're not reaching new customers."* She has requested a website redesign budget from Marcus three times. She has not received a formal response.

**Patricia Chu — Finance Director**
Patricia works two days per week as a part-time contractor. She manages financial reporting, the annual budget, and vendor contracts. She is not convinced that a technology investment will solve the booking problem. *"We've had 18% growth years without any platform. Something specific changed. I'd want to understand what changed before I approve a build that costs six figures."* She has frozen the technology budget pending a formal business case with clear ROI projections.

**Sam Torres — Lead Guide Representative**
Sam has been a professional guide for 8 years and has worked with TrailBlaze for 4 of them. He is the informal liaison between the contract guides and the operations team — there is no formal guide representative role. He is concerned about guide communication. *"I find out about assignments 24 to 48 hours before a trip. That's okay for me because I know the routes well. But newer guides don't know what gear a specific group needs, what the pacing should be, or who has a medical condition. I've had trips where the customer list changed after I was confirmed and nobody told me."*

## What You Are Walking Into

No business analyst has been assigned to TrailBlaze yet. That changes today.

You are stepping into a company where:
- Leadership cannot agree on the root cause of the problem
- A solution has already been implied publicly (Marcus has shown interest in a new platform)
- The finance budget is frozen
- The person with the most operational knowledge (Rachel) has not shared her full view
- There is no data infrastructure — most of what the company knows is in people's heads and a Google Sheet

Your first job is not to fix anything. It is to understand what is actually happening before anyone spends money or writes a line of code.
