# TrailBlaze Adventures — Personas

What this is:   Customer and guide personas for the TrailBlaze case study — written as narrative profiles so participants can reason about needs and value from a real person's perspective, not just a label.

## Persona 1 — Alex Chen, "The Weekend Warrior"

**Age:** 29 | **Location:** Charlotte, NC | **Occupation:** Digital marketing specialist at a mid-sized tech company

**The backstory:**
Alex works 50-hour weeks and spends most of them at a desk. He started hiking seriously about two years ago after a stressful quarter at work — a coworker invited him on a guided trip and he was immediately hooked. He has done four TrailBlaze trips so far, three of them solo (where he joined a group of strangers) and one with two friends from work.

**How Alex books a trip (today):**
Alex tried to book a trip last month. He sent a message through the TrailBlaze Instagram DM on a Sunday afternoon asking about availability for a specific weekend in October. By Tuesday he hadn't heard back, so he sent an email. He got a response Thursday. By that point he had already booked with a competitor.

*"I wasn't even unhappy with the experience. I just needed an answer faster. If there's a way to book it myself without waiting, I'll use it."*

**What Alex actually needs:**
- To find available trips that match his fitness level and preferred dates without a back-and-forth conversation
- To book in 5 minutes or less, ideally on his phone, ideally on a Sunday when he's making plans for the month
- To know who his guide will be before he shows up — not because he'll research them extensively, but because it reduces uncertainty
- To have the itinerary somewhere he can find it, not buried in an email thread

**What Alex doesn't say out loud but is clearly true:**
Alex has four apps on his phone he uses to book things in under 3 minutes. He doesn't consciously compare TrailBlaze to those apps, but he measures every booking experience against them. An email-and-wait process doesn't feel broken to him — it just feels slower than what he's used to. He will choose a competitor that is marginally worse but frictionless over a better experience that requires effort.

**How Alex defines success:** He completed a booking without calling anyone, has the itinerary on his phone, and knows the guide's name. That's it.

**ECBA relevance:** Alex's need is about self-serve access and speed — but that need could be met in multiple ways (a booking platform, a WhatsApp bot, a simplified email auto-response). His need is not "build a mobile app." His need is "let me complete this without waiting."

## Persona 2 — Maria Reyes, "The Family Planner"

**Age:** 42 | **Location:** Raleigh, NC | **Occupation:** Assistant principal at a public elementary school

**The backstory:**
Maria's family does one outdoor trip together every summer — it's been a tradition since her kids were young. Her two children are now 13 and 16. Her husband is enthusiastic but disorganized, so Maria does all the planning. She found TrailBlaze two years ago when her kids wanted something more structured than a self-guided trail but less expensive than a resort package. They've done one TrailBlaze weekend trip. It was good but came with a lot of uncertainty she had to manage herself.

**How Maria booked her trip (last time):**
Maria emailed TrailBlaze in March for a July trip. She asked: (1) Is the moderate Pisgah loop suitable for a 13-year-old? (2) Is the guide familiar with first aid for minors? (3) Can we confirm the group will be family groups only, not adult-only? She got an email back that answered questions 1 and 3 but not 2. She followed up. The guide information came back 4 days later and did not include any credentials or first aid certification. She booked anyway but felt uneasy until the trip.

*"The trip was lovely. The guide was wonderful — she had wilderness first aid certification and had been doing this for 12 years. I just wish I had known that upfront. If I could have seen her bio and qualifications before I booked, I would have felt so much better."*

**What Maria actually needs:**
- Safety transparency — guide credentials, certifications, years of experience — visible before booking
- Age-appropriate trip filtering — she wants to know immediately whether a trip is appropriate for a 13-year-old, not after asking
- A clear pre-trip checklist — what to bring, what to wear, what to expect at check-in
- Reliability — if she has confirmed a date with her kids and husband, a last-minute cancellation is not a minor inconvenience; it's a family planning failure
- Some way to pay that isn't Venmo or a personal check — she's fine with Venmo personally, but she has to justify family expenses to her husband and *"I paid via Venmo"* doesn't feel like a real transaction confirmation

**What Maria doesn't say out loud but is clearly true:**
Maria is making a safety decision, not just a booking decision. She would pay 15–20% more for a company that made her feel confident about the safety profile upfront. She will absolutely not return if anything goes wrong and she felt the information she needed wasn't available.

**How Maria defines success:** She has a confirmation email that looks like a real invoice, guide bio with certifications, a packing checklist, and nobody calls her to change the plan less than a week out.

**ECBA relevance:** Maria's need overlaps with Alex's (self-serve, frictionless) but is fundamentally different — her need is about safety confidence and pre-trip transparency. A booking platform that solves Alex's need might not solve Maria's. Requirements must be written for both personas, not just one.

## Persona 3 — Sam Torres, "The Lead Guide"

**Age:** 36 | **Location:** Weaverville, NC (near Asheville) | **Occupation:** Full-time professional guide; part-time outdoor skills instructor

**The backstory:**
Sam has been a certified wilderness guide for 8 years. He holds Wilderness First Responder (WFR) certification and is trained in swift-water rescue. He has worked with TrailBlaze for 4 years and is one of the most experienced guides on the contracted roster. He takes about 40 trips per year through TrailBlaze — roughly 30–35% of his annual income.

He became the informal "lead guide rep" because he's been around long enough that other guides come to him with operational questions. There is no formal representation structure — he has no official title and is not compensated for coordination work.

**What Sam's assignment process looks like today:**
Sam gets a text from Keisha: *"Saturday the 18th, Black Balsam Knob, 6 pax, moderate. You available?"* If he says yes, that's it until a second text 24–48 hours before the trip with a roster (usually: first names and city of origin, nothing else). He finds out about any changes — customer additions, cancellations, medical notes — if Keisha remembers to tell him.

*"Last June I had a trip where a customer had a severe peanut allergy. It was in their booking form. Nobody told me. I carry an EpiPen for exactly this reason, but I only knew to check because the customer mentioned it at the trailhead. That can't be how this works."*

**What Sam actually needs:**
- Assignment confirmation with sufficient lead time — at minimum 72 hours, ideally a week for complex groups
- A roster with relevant information: group size, fitness level notes, any medical flags, gear the group is bringing
- A single reliable way to communicate with his group before the trip — right now he either has no contact info or has to ask Keisha to pass messages
- Notification if anything changes — customer additions, cancellations, date shifts — not discovered at the trailhead
- On-time payment — Sam gets paid 3–5 days post-trip; there have been two payment delays in the past year that took 3 weeks to resolve

**What Sam doesn't say out loud but is clearly true:**
Sam is loyal to TrailBlaze because of the quality of the routes and the customer base, not because the operations are smooth. If a competitor offered similar routes with better guide communications and reliable payment, he would seriously consider shifting work. Three of the five guides who left in the past two years left because of the notification and payment issues, not because of the customers.

**How Sam defines success:** He knows about his assignment at least 72 hours out, the roster has the information he needs, and nobody surprises him at the trailhead.

**ECBA relevance:** Sam is a stakeholder who is typically missed in the first pass of a stakeholder analysis because he's not in the organizational chart. He's a contracted service provider, not an employee. But the product TrailBlaze delivers — a safe, well-guided trip — depends entirely on him. If the BA misses Sam, the requirements miss the guide assignment workflow, and the pilot results will show exactly that gap.
