# Facilitator Guide — v2: Bi-Weekly, With Session B

--- DOCUMENT GUIDE ---
What this is:   Explicit facilitator instructions for the v2 format — bi-weekly schedule with separate Session A and Session B. Covers pre-series setup, session-by-session logistics, scripted transitions, and how to handle common problems.
Who it's for:   Facilitator only.
When to use it: Review before the series starts. Re-read the relevant session section the night before each meeting.
Restricted:     Yes — do not share with participants.
--- END GUIDE ---

## Before the Series Starts

### Week −3 or Earlier: SME Pipeline
You need 5 confirmed speakers before you run Session 1A. Do not start the series without at least Sessions 1B and 2B confirmed. Use this checklist:

- [ ] Identify 5 candidate SMEs (see SME profiles in the base plan — one per session B)
- [ ] Send initial outreach with the full 10-week calendar attached — SMEs need to see all dates upfront
- [ ] Confirm each speaker at least 3 weeks before their session
- [ ] Send the SME prep guide (`etn/ECBA_CaseStudy/Facilitator/Session_B_SME_Prep_Guide.md`) at confirmation
- [ ] Follow up 48 hours before each Session B with logistics (link, time, what to expect)

**If you are still recruiting Session 3B when you run Session 2A, that is normal.** You will always be one step ahead of your own speaker calendar. Build this into your weekly rhythm.

**If an SME cancels within 72 hours:** Run the Session B exercise yourself as a facilitated group debrief. Tell participants: "Our speaker had a conflict. I'm going to run this session as a structured case review instead." Use the Session B exercise prompt from the base plan and ask the questions the SME would have asked. It will not be identical, but it will be better than canceling.

### Week −1: Session 0 Distribution
- Distribute the Session 0 Onboarding Handout (`ECBA_CaseStudyv2/Session0_Onboarding_Handout.md`)
- Send a follow-up message 2 days later: "Quick reminder — Session 1A is [date/time]. Required reading: BABOK Ch. 1 and Ch. 2, Sections 2.1–2.4. See you there."
- Confirm meeting link, room, or location is working

## Running Session A (All Five Sessions)

Session A is 90 minutes. Follow the base plan phase table for each session. The v2-specific notes below apply across all five.

### Pre-session (arrive 15 minutes early)
- [ ] Distribute or display: Workshop Handout, relevant template for the exercise (see base plan per session)
- [ ] Display the opening slide while participants arrive — do not stand at the front waiting; circulate and greet
- [ ] Have Session 1 Facilitator Answers or equivalent ready for the practice round

### Opening (0:00–0:10)
**Sessions 2A–5A open with a homework share-out, not a cold ARCS open.**

Say: *"Let's start with homework. I want to hear what you brought. [Name], what did you come up with? Give me the one sentence that matters most."*

Do not call on the most confident person first. Call on someone mid-confidence — they set the baseline for the room. The confident person can sharpen the conversation; the quieter person will follow if the first answer is not intimidating.

Take 2–3 responses, then say: *"Here's what I notice across these — [name 1 strong element]. Here's what we're going to need to push on today — [name the gap that the new session content will address]. That's where we're going next."*

This bridge (3–4 sentences) connects the homework to the new content and creates the ARCS relevance beat without a formal script.

**If no one did the homework:** Do not shame or pause. Say: *"Okay, let's use the time differently. I'll give you 3 minutes right now to write one sentence — your best guess at [homework deliverable] based on what you remember from last session. Go."* Then run the share-out with those responses. You lose 3 minutes and gain a room that is engaged instead of embarrassed.

### Concept instruction (0:10–0:35, varies by session)
- Use the TrailBlaze anchor for every concept — do not explain a concept in the abstract and then apply it. Apply it simultaneously.
- Pause at every checkpoint question in the base plan. These are not optional. Read the question aloud, give 30 seconds of silence, then ask for a vote or a show of hands. Reveal the answer after you hear from 2–3 people.
- **If the group answers the checkpoint correctly:** Say *"Good. Here's why the wrong options are wrong — that distinction matters on the exam."* Spend 60 seconds on distractors.
- **If the group answers incorrectly:** Do not move on. Say *"Let's look at this more carefully. The question is asking about [specific concept] — where are people getting pulled toward [wrong option]?"* Spend 2–3 minutes here. This is more valuable than staying on schedule.

### Group exercise (0:43–1:05, varies by session)
- Split groups before giving the prompt — decide groups yourself (count off, or assign in advance). Do not let participants self-select every time; the same people will cluster.
- Read the exercise prompt aloud after groups are seated. Then say: *"You have [time]. Assign a scribe and a presenter. Go."*
- Circulate every 4–5 minutes. Do not hover over one group.
- **Prompts to use when a group is stuck:**
  - "What does TrailBlaze actually need here — not what they're asking for?"
  - "Who in the stakeholder list would disagree with what you just wrote, and why?"
  - "If you had to cut this in half, what stays?"
- Give a 3-minute warning, then a 1-minute warning. Say these out loud to the room, not just to one group.

### Share-out (1:05–1:15, varies by session)
- Each group gets 2–3 minutes to present. Keep time visibly.
- After each group: give one specific thing that worked and one specific thing to sharpen. **"Good" is not feedback.** "Your problem statement has a clear symptom — what it's missing is a measurable baseline" is feedback.
- **In v2, share-outs are the primary quality gate.** Session B will add friction, but it will not fix a fundamentally weak artifact. If you see a group with a major error, name it now: *"Before we move on — there's something here I want to flag."*

### Practice round (1:15–1:23)
- Display one question at a time (4 questions, 2 minutes each)
- 1 minute individual — enforce silence; no discussion until you say so
- 30 seconds: ask for answers; take 2–3 responses
- 30 seconds: reveal correct answer and explain why each distractor fails

**If you are running 5 minutes late:** Do 2 questions in the room, assign the other 2 as homework alongside the regular homework set. Say: *"We're short on time — I'm adding these two to your homework. Do them before Session B, not the night of."*

### Close (1:23–1:30)
- Read the homework prompt aloud (do not just refer to a slide)
- Say: *"You have one week before Session B. The homework is due before that session — not after. Bring your best current version, not a polished final. [SME name] will engage directly with what you have."*
- Name the stretch question from Session B that will open the following Session A share-out: *"At the end of Session B, [SME] will close with a stretch question. It will feed directly into the Session [X+1] share-out. Write your answer down when it's asked — you will use it."*
- Send a 2-minute survey link (or distribute paper survey before people leave)

## Running Session B (All Five Sessions)

Session B is 60 minutes with an SME. Your role in Session B is logistics and time management, not instruction.

### Day before Session B
- Confirm the SME is still available and has the prep guide
- Send the SME: the session's B exercise format, the stretch question, and 2–3 sentences on where the cohort is ("The group is solid on X; they're struggling to distinguish Y from Z")
- Have participants' homework artifacts ready to share if the SME wants to review them

### Session B run-of-show (60 minutes)

**0:00–0:05 — Setup**

Say: *"Welcome back. Tonight is Session B — we're not introducing new content. [SME name] is here to add friction to the work you've been doing. That means they're going to push back, challenge your reasoning, and ask questions a real stakeholder would ask. Your job is to defend your position using what you know. Ready?"*

Introduce the SME: name, current role, one relevant thing about their background. 3 sentences maximum — do not read a biography.

**0:05–0:40 — SME exercise**

Hand off to the SME. Your role: watch the clock. At 30 minutes, hold up a visual signal for the SME (not verbal — don't interrupt). At 35 minutes, if the exercise is still running, say quietly: *"We need to start wrapping the exercise in about 5 minutes."*

Do not intervene in the exercise unless a participant is being unfairly targeted or the discussion has gone completely off-topic. In both cases, step in directly: *"Let me redirect us — [reframe the question or redirect the group]."*

**0:40–0:52 — Debrief**

Say to the SME: *"[Name], let's step back — tell the group what you were actually doing in there."*

Then let the SME speak. Do not add to or interpret their debrief while they're giving it. After they finish, ask the group: *"What did you hear? What surprised you?"* Take 2 responses.

**0:52–1:00 — Stretch question + close**

The SME asks the stretch question (printed on their prep guide). Say: *"Take 30 seconds and write down your answer. You will use this at the next Session A share-out."*

After 30 seconds, ask 1–2 people to share their current thinking (not their final answer — they can refine it). This previews the stakes for the next A session.

Thank the SME. Send participants the survey. Close.

## Common Problems and Responses

### "I didn't do the reading."
Do not let this derail the session. Concept instruction is designed to stand alone. When this comes up in a share-out: *"That's okay — let's work with what you have. What does your instinct say, even without the reading?"*

### "I didn't finish the homework."
At Session B: *"Bring what you have. [SME] works with work-in-progress — that's the point."* Do not reschedule or delay the session.

### A group finishes the exercise in 10 minutes.
Assign a second prompt immediately: *"Good. Now do it again — but assume the budget for Option 1 was just cut in half. What changes?"* Fast completion usually means the group took a shallow pass. This second pass surfaces the depth.

### A group has not started after 10 minutes.
Walk over and ask one person directly: *"What's the first thing you'd write on this? Just one word or phrase — go."* Once one person commits to something, the group usually follows.

### The SME goes long and there is no time for the stretch question.
Cut the debrief short (not the exercise). Say: *"We're out of time — I'm going to give you the stretch question in writing. It will be your homework before the next session."* Send it by email or message that night.

### Attendance drops from session to session.
Address it directly with the group — not individually. At the open of Session [N]: *"A few people have missed a session. I want to name that — this case study builds. If you missed a session, review the slides and the homework prompt from that session before today. You won't have the full context, but you'll have enough to contribute."*

## Timing Recovery Cheat Sheet

If you fall behind, cut in this order — least damage first:

| Phase to cut | Time saved | What you lose |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Homework share-out: 10 min → 7 min | 3 min | Some groups don't present |
| Guided application: 8 min → 5 min | 3 min | Less modeling before group exercise |
| Share-out: 8 min → 5 min | 3 min | Less feedback per group |
| Practice round: 8 min → 4 min (2 Qs) | 4 min | Assign remaining 2 Qs as homework |
| Close: 6 min → 3 min | 3 min | Survey skipped (send link by email) |

Do not cut the group exercise. Do not cut the practice round entirely — 2 questions in the room is better than 0.
