# Facilitator Guide — v3: Bi-Weekly, Without Session B

--- DOCUMENT GUIDE ---
What this is:   Explicit facilitator instructions for the v3 format — bi-weekly A sessions only, no SME deep dives. Covers pre-series setup, session-by-session logistics, scripted transitions, how to run share-outs as friction replacements, and common problem handling.
Who it's for:   Facilitator only.
When to use it: Review before the series starts. Re-read the relevant section the night before each session.
Restricted:     Yes — do not share with participants.
--- END GUIDE ---

## What Is Different About v3

In v1 and v2, Session B does the friction work: a practitioner pushes back on participants' thinking at the moment when they are most confident and most wrong. In v3, that session does not exist. The friction has to come from two other places:

1. **The homework challenge tasks** — each homework set includes a scenario where the participant's work is pushed back on. Participants do this alone, in writing, between sessions.
2. **Your share-out facilitation** — you are the only person in the room who can apply structured pressure to weak work. The share-out is where you do it.

If you run gentle, affirming share-outs in v3, participants will not encounter the friction that sharpens their reasoning before the exam. The probing questions in this guide are not optional — they are what Session B would have done.

## Running Session A (All Five Sessions)

Session A is 90 minutes. Follow the base plan phase table for each session. The v3-specific notes below apply across all five.

### Pre-session (arrive 15 minutes early)
- [ ] Distribute or display: Workshop Handout, relevant template for the exercise (see base plan per session)
- [ ] Have the session's probing questions ready (see section below) — printed or open on your device
- [ ] Have the challenge task for this session's homework ready to read aloud at close

### Opening — Homework Share-Out (Sessions 2A–5A, 0:00–0:10)

**The share-out is the most important 10 minutes in v3.** In v1 and v2, a weaker share-out is compensated for by Session B. In v3, it is not.

Open every session (2A–5A) with: *"Let's start with the challenge task. I want to hear how it went. Who wants to go first?"*

If no one volunteers immediately, say: *"[Name] — give me your one sentence. What was the situation in the challenge task asking you to do?"* (Pick someone confident.) Once one person speaks, others follow.

**Standard share-out structure:**
- Ask for the participant's response to the challenge task first (not the homework artifact)
- Ask one probing question from the list below (pick the one that applies to their response)
- Ask 1–2 others if they had a different answer and why
- Transition: *"Here's what I'm seeing — [name the most common gap]. That's exactly what we're going to dig into today."*

**Session-specific probing questions for the challenge task share-out:**

*After Session 1A — BACCM pushback response:*
- "You said to use the BACCM to push back. Walk me through exactly what you would say to the CEO. Not the concept — the words."
- "What happens if the CEO agrees with your framing? What is your next move?"
- "Is there a scenario where the CEO is right and the BA should stop pushing back?"

*After Session 2A — Interview notes challenge:*
- "You wrote three follow-up questions. Which one would you ask first, and why?"
- "The constraint-based question — what does the answer tell you about whether the problem is solvable?"
- "If the Head of Operations refuses to meet again, what do you do with those notes?"

*After Session 3A — Requirement verification challenge:*
- "Show me the before and after for one of your requirements. Read me both versions."
- "The revised version — how would you verify it in an acceptance test? Be specific."
- "What would happen if you shipped something that met this requirement exactly as written but still didn't solve the problem?"

*After Session 4A — CEO objection response:*
- "You said the current analysis is different from six months ago. What specifically is different? Point to your RTM."
- "What do you do if the CEO says 'I'll need to see that in writing before the next board meeting'?"
- "What remaining doubt do you address, and what remaining doubt do you leave open?"

### Share-out — Artifact Feedback (1:05–1:15, varies by session)

This share-out covers the in-session artifact (not the challenge task — that was the opening). Apply probing questions here because there is no Session B to do it later.

**After each group presents, do not just affirm. Ask one of the following:**

*Session 1A — Problem statement:*
- "Where is the measurable baseline in this statement? If you took a measurement today, what would you measure?"
- "Which leadership stakeholder would disagree with this framing most, and what would they say?"

*Session 2A — BA approach recommendation:*
- "You chose [approach]. What is the scenario where that approach fails?"
- "Which stakeholder did you decide to engage last, and why?"

*Session 3A — Requirements conversion:*
- "Read me your weakest requirement. Now tell me: how would you verify it?"
- "If scope changes and one of these requirements has to go, which one and why?"

*Session 4A — Solution recommendation + RTM:*
- "You recommended [option]. Your RTM traces [N] requirements to it. Are there requirements in your list that your recommended option does NOT satisfy? What happens to those?"
- "If a new stakeholder walks in tomorrow who wasn't in your original list, does your recommendation hold?"

*Session 5A — KPI analysis:*
- "Which missed KPI was the BA most responsible for? Not the technology team — the BA."
- "If the pilot had hit all targets, would that mean the BA's work was correct? Explain."

### Practice Round (1:15–1:23)
Same as v2 guide. Do not cut below 2 questions.

### Close — Homework and Challenge Task (1:23–1:30)

Read the homework assignment aloud, then read the challenge task aloud. Do not just refer to a slide or document.

Say: *"The challenge task is the most important part of your homework this cycle. It puts your work in a situation where someone pushes back on it. Write a real response — not what you think is the right answer in theory, but what you would actually say or do. We'll open with it next session."*

Name the specific person's response you want to hear first at the next share-out, if you already know who is likely to bring interesting thinking. This increases accountability without singling anyone out in a negative way.

## Optional Third Checkpoint (When to Use It)

The base plan includes 2 checkpoint questions per session. Add the third checkpoint from the plan when:
- The group completes the guided application in under 5 minutes with no questions
- More than half the group answers both standard checkpoints correctly without discussion
- The energy in the room is compliant rather than engaged

**How to add it:** After the second checkpoint, say: *"One more — this one is harder."* Read the optional checkpoint from the plan. Give 45 seconds, then ask for answers. The optional checkpoints are written to surface a deeper distinction that confident participants often get wrong.

If you do not see those signals, skip the third checkpoint and use the time for the group exercise.

## Common Problems and Responses

### "The challenge task was too hard / I didn't know what to write."
This is a signal that the concept from the previous session did not land or the participant did not complete the reading. Respond: *"That's useful to know. Let's work through it together — what did you understand about [concept]? Start there."* Do not skip the challenge task discussion because someone found it hard — hard is the point.

### A group's artifact is missing the core concept entirely.
Do not wait for share-out to address it. During the exercise, if you see a major error, say to that group: *"Pause for a second — look at what you've written here. Does this connect back to [concept from instruction]? Walk me through your thinking."* This gives them a chance to self-correct before presenting.

### Participants start asking "what would Session B have covered?"
Answer directly: *"Session B in the full-format series includes a practitioner who adds friction to your work — an interview, a requirements challenge, a solution pushback. In this format, the challenge tasks do that work. Your responses to those tasks are how you build the same skill."*

### Participation drops off in sessions 3–5.
By session 3, participants know the pattern and may coast. Disrupt the pattern: change the group composition, add the optional checkpoint, call on quieter participants directly for the share-out probing questions. Do not run the same dynamic 5 sessions in a row.

### Someone brings a genuinely excellent artifact to share-out.
Use it. Say: *"I want to spend a minute on this one — this is what [concept] looks like when it's applied correctly. Here's specifically why:"* Naming excellence gives the room a target, not just a correction.

## Timing Recovery Cheat Sheet

Cut in this order:

| Phase to cut | Time saved | What you lose |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Challenge task share-out: 10 min → 7 min | 3 min | Fewer responses heard |
| Concept instruction: tighten by reducing 1 checkpoint | 2 min | Slightly less mid-concept calibration |
| Guided application: 8 min → 5 min | 3 min | Less modeling |
| Artifact share-out: 8 min → 5 min | 3 min | Less probing per group |
| Practice round: 8 min → 4 min (2 Qs) | 4 min | Assign remaining Qs as homework |

Do not cut the challenge task share-out entirely. It is the only friction mechanism in this format. If time is short, run 7 minutes of challenge task share-out and 5 minutes of artifact share-out instead of 10 and 8.
