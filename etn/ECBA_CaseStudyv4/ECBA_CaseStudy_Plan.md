## Overview

This is the **bi-weekly combined** format. Five 2-hour sessions run every two weeks, completing in approximately **10 weeks**. Session A content (concept instruction, guided application, group exercise, practice round) runs first. Session B content (practitioner challenge, debrief, stretch question) follows in the second half of the same meeting.

One SME (or the facilitator) runs the practitioner segment across all five sessions.

## Combined Session Run-of-Show

Each session follows this structure. Total: 120 minutes.

| Phase | Activity | Time |
|-------|----------|------|
| **A — Open** | ARCS open (cold or homework share-out, depending on session) | 10 min |
| **A — Concepts** | Domain instruction with TrailBlaze anchoring | 22–25 min |
| **A — Guided application** | Facilitator-led partial example | 8 min |
| **A — Group exercise** | Participants produce the session artifact | 20–22 min |
| **A — Share-out** | Group presentations + facilitator feedback | 8 min |
| **A — Practice round** | 4 timed multiple choice questions | 8 min |
| **(Transition)** | 5-minute break; commit prompt distributed | 5 min |
| **B — Commit prompt** | Participants write their position in one sentence; SME is introduced | 3 min |
| **B — Practitioner exercise** | SME-led friction challenge (session-specific format) | 25–28 min |
| **B — Debrief** | SME steps out of character; open reflection | 8 min |
| **B — Stretch + close** | Stretch question assigned; homework distributed; 2-min survey | 5 min |

**Total: 122 minutes** — plan for a 2-hour block. The 5-minute break is structural (gives participants and the SME a moment to reset before the B segment begins) and should not be cut.

## The Commit Prompt

The commit prompt replaces the 2-week gap that separates A and B in the base series. It forces participants to commit to a position before the SME interaction begins, creating the productive tension that the gap would otherwise generate.

**How it works:**
- During the 5-minute break, distribute a half-sheet (physical or slide) with a single sentence prompt.
- Participants write their answer — one sentence only. No discussion.
- The SME is introduced and begins the B segment by engaging directly with what the group wrote.

**Commit prompts by session:**

| Session | Commit Prompt |
|---------|--------------|
| 1 | "In one sentence: what is the real problem at TrailBlaze right now?" |
| 2 | "In one sentence: which root cause are you most confident about, and why?" |
| 3 | "In one sentence: which of your raw needs is the hardest to turn into a verifiable requirement, and what makes it hard?" |
| 4 | "In one sentence: which solution option do you recommend, and what is the one requirement it must satisfy?" |
| 5 | "In one sentence: what is the single most important thing the BA got right in this engagement?" |

The SME reads several responses aloud (anonymized), picks 2–3 that represent different positions, and uses them as the starting point for the exercise. This makes the participants' own thinking the raw material for the practitioner challenge.

## Session-by-Session Practitioner Segments

### Session 1 — BACCM Workshop (25 min)
**Exercise:** SME plays themselves — a BA who has just walked into a messy engagement. Participants share their one-sentence problem statement (commit prompt). SME responds to each with one clarifying challenge: *"What does the BACCM say about that?"* Group works to defend or revise. SME closes with a real example of a BA who walked into a situation with a pre-decided solution and what happened.

**Commit prompt bridge:** The SME opens with: *"I read your answers. Here is what I notice about the room."* Then picks two contrasting responses and asks: *"Which of these is a problem statement and which is a solution assumption? How do you tell the difference?"*

---

### Session 2 — Mock Stakeholder Interview (25 min)
**Exercise:** SME plays the Head of Operations or Marketing Lead, giving deliberately incomplete or conflicting answers. Group practices live elicitation. After 15 minutes the SME steps out of character and debriefs: *"Here is what I was withholding, and here is why people really do that in interviews."*

**Commit prompt bridge:** SME opens with: *"You told me which root cause you're most confident about. I'm the person who disagrees with you. Convince me."*

---

### Session 3 — Requirements Conversion (25 min)
**Exercise:** Participants share their hardest-to-write requirement (from commit prompt). SME works through it live, asking: *"How would you verify this? What happens if I interpret it differently than you intended? What scope does this open that you didn't plan for?"* 2–3 requirements worked through in sequence.

**Commit prompt bridge:** SME opens by naming the pattern they see across the responses: *"Most of you chose [X type of need] as the hard one. Here is why that type is hard, and here is the question you should ask yourself before you finish the sentence."*

---

### Session 4 — Solution Challenge (25 min)
**Exercise:** SME plays the CEO or an executive sponsor, reviewing the group's solution recommendation and pushing back with genuine objections — cost, political history, timeline, "we already tried something like this." Participants defend using ECBA language and their RTM. SME closes with how solution decisions actually get made and what BA documentation actually influences them.

**Commit prompt bridge:** SME opens with: *"You recommended [solution]. I read it. Here is my first question as the person who has to sign off."* (SME improvises based on the most common recommendation in the commit prompts.)

---

### Session 5 — Post-Pilot Retrospective (25 min)
**Exercise:** SME shares a real story — a deployment where some targets were hit and others were missed. Participants map the SME's story to the TrailBlaze pilot data: *"Where does your experience match ours? Where does it differ? What did the BA control and what didn't they?"* Closes with the SME answering the stretch question as a personal reflection.

**Commit prompt bridge:** SME opens with: *"You each named something the BA got right. I'm going to tell you what I got wrong. Then I want to hear whether yours still holds."*

## SME Briefing Notes for v4

Share the following with the SME before the series begins. Reference the full SME prep guide at `etn/ECBA_CaseStudy/Facilitator/Session_B_SME_Prep_Guide.md` for the standard briefing.

**v4-specific additions:**

1. You will run all five practitioner segments. The cohort will know you across 10 weeks. Use that relationship — call back to earlier sessions when the case allows it.

2. You will receive the commit prompt responses during the 5-minute break before your segment. Scan them quickly and pick 2–3 contrasting answers to anchor your opening. You do not need to read all of them.

3. Your job is not to lecture — it is to create productive friction. The participants have just spent 70 minutes learning and producing work. They feel competent. Your job is to introduce one well-placed doubt and let them work through it.

4. The debrief (8 minutes) matters as much as the exercise. Tell them what you were doing — what friction you were applying and why. Name the real-world equivalent. This converts experience into transferable insight.

5. The stretch question closes every session. Answer it as a personal reflection, not a textbook answer. This is the moment participants remember.
